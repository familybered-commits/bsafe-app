Public pensions promise a ruler. Bitcoin delivers scarcity.

How recurring purchases (DCA) into a fixed-supply asset can become the foundation of a retirement plan that's truly your own — independent of formulas, reforms, and the future purchasing power of a government benefit.

📚 Retirement & Bitcoin ⏱ 8 min read 🟠 bsafebitcoin.org

If you're a salaried employee or contribute as a self-employed worker, a slice of your monthly income is, by law, set aside for the public pension system. The promise is simple: contribute during your working years and, in old age, receive a benefit that sustains your standard of living. In theory, it's a pact between generations. In practice, it's a public promise, paid in fiat currency, calculated under rules that have already been changed multiple times — and that depend on the government's future ability to honor the commitment with a currency that loses value every year.

This article isn't about abandoning your public pension system — it's about understanding its structural limitations and presenting DCA (Dollar Cost Averaging) into Bitcoin as a complementary layer of retirement planning, built on an asset whose rules can't be changed by decree, reform, or fiscal crisis.

What DCA actually means

DCA stands for recurring purchases of a fixed amount, at regular intervals, regardless of the asset's price at the time of purchase. Instead of trying to guess "the best moment" to buy Bitcoin — something nobody can do consistently — you buy, say, $50 every month, always on the same date, for years.

1

Fixed amount, fixed frequency. You set an amount that fits your budget (e.g., $20, $50, $100 a month) and a date — every month, no exceptions, without waiting for "money left over."

2

You buy more when it's cheap, less when it's expensive. Since the amount is fixed, when Bitcoin's price drops, the same money buys more satoshis — and vice versa. This automatically smooths your average entry price.

3

It removes the anxiety of "perfect timing." You don't need to nail the bottom or predict the top. Long-term discipline replaces the attempt at short-term guesswork.

4

You accumulate in a fixed-supply asset. Every satoshi bought today is a fraction of a total that will never exceed 21 million units — unlike any pension fund or fiat currency, whose "supply" can grow according to the political needs of the moment.

The structural problems of fiat-based pension systems

To understand why DCA into Bitcoin works as a complement — not just as a "risky investment" — you need to look honestly at the structural characteristics of public pension systems.

1. Rules that change over time

The benefit calculation formula, the minimum age, the required contribution period, and the benefit cap have already been changed by multiple reforms over recent decades, and nothing stops them from changing again. Whoever is contributing today is building a right under rules that may be different by the time they go to use it.

2. The benefit's real value depends on the currency

Even if the nominal value of the benefit gets adjusted, what matters is the purchasing power of that value at the moment it's received. As we saw in the "infinite vs. scarce money" principle: a currency whose supply can grow indefinitely tends to buy less and less over the decades. A benefit "preserved" in nominal terms isn't necessarily one that preserves purchasing power.

3. Dependence on a pay-as-you-go system

The dominant model for public pensions is pay-as-you-go: today's workers fund today's retirees. That means the system's sustainability depends directly on the ratio between active contributors and beneficiaries — a ratio that, in many countries, is becoming structurally more challenging over time.

The central point

None of this means the public pension system will "collapse" overnight, or that contributing is pointless — it's an important, and for most people mandatory, social protection system. The point is different: putting 100% of your retirement expectations into a single promise, paid in a currency that's being diluted, calculated by rules that change, means concentrating your entire future on variables you don't control.

DCA into Bitcoin as a complementary layer

This is where DCA into Bitcoin fits in — not as a replacement for your public pension, but as a second leg of your long-term strategy, with complementary, and in several respects opposite, characteristics:

Fiat Pension System

  • Calculation rules already changed multiple times
  • Benefit paid in a currency that loses purchasing power
  • Depends on the ratio of contributors to beneficiaries
  • Management and rules set by a third party (the government)
  • Eligibility conditioned on contribution time and age

DCA Into Bitcoin

  • Issuance rules fixed since 2009, never changed
  • Wealth held in an asset with a maximum supply of 21 million
  • Doesn't depend on a ratio between generations
  • Custody and rules under the user's own control
  • Available at any time, with no minimum age

Combining the two isn't a coincidence: one is a collective safety net, subject to a country's rules and fiscal health. The other is a personal store of value, built satoshi by satoshi, under rules that no government, party, or crisis can renegotiate.

Why the long-term horizon favors DCA

Retirement planning, by definition, is a decades-long exercise. That's exactly the kind of horizon where Bitcoin's short-term volatility loses relevance and the underlying thesis — absolute scarcity against growing monetary issuance — gains weight. A purchase made today doesn't need to "pay off" next month; it's one piece of a mosaic that only makes sense viewed from a distance, over an interval of 10, 15, 20 years.

A fiat pension is a third-party promise, paid in a currency that's being diluted. DCA into Bitcoin is building your own wealth, in an asset that can't be diluted. One doesn't replace the other — but ignoring the second means betting your entire future on a single ruler.

Tracking your "sovereign retirement plan"

Just like with any retirement plan, what matters isn't only contributing — it's tracking the progress. How much BTC have you accumulated so far? What was your average entry price, in USD and BRL? How is this strategy performing against other store-of-value assets, like gold or major stock indices?

Visualizing these numbers over time turns DCA from an "act of faith" into a measurable process — just like any retirement account statement, but with the difference that the underlying asset has a fixed supply and is under your own custody.

What to do with this information

Continuing to contribute to your public pension system, when mandatory or strategically advantageous, while also building, in a disciplined and recurring way, a Bitcoin reserve via DCA — these aren't mutually exclusive choices. They're two layers of the same goal — security in the future — resting on very different foundations: one on a collective, fiat-based promise, the other on a mathematical, scarce protocol.

This isn't investment advice, nor a promise of returns — it's an invitation to reflect on the structural incentives of each system. It's up to each person to study, understand the risks, and make their own decisions responsibly.
