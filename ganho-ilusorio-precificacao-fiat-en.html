Your investment returns 12%? Find out what's really left.

When your return is measured in the same currency that's losing value, the gain on paper can be an illusion. See how the crooked ruler of real inflation distorts every return calculation.

📚 Financial Education ⏱ 7 min read 🟠 bsafebitcoin.org

Imagine you have a wooden ruler. It reads 30 centimeters and you use it to measure everything: furniture, walls, how tall your kid is getting. Now imagine that ruler quietly shrinks a little every year — but the numbers printed on it never change. What you measure still reads "30 cm." But the actual object got smaller.

That's exactly what happens when you use fiat currency to measure how your investments are performing. The ruler is shrinking. And most people never notice.

The unit-of-measurement problem

Every financial result is a comparison: how much came in, how much went out, how much is left. But for that comparison to mean anything, it needs to be made with a stable unit of measurement. This is where today's monetary system has a structural problem that rarely shows up on bank statements.

When your fixed-income fund returns 12% a year and official inflation says prices rose 5%, common sense concludes: "I really made 7%." But that math has at least two serious problems.

The first is mathematical: you can't subtract percentages directly. Real return should be calculated using the Fisher equation — but that's just the start. The second problem runs deeper: the official inflation index isn't your family's inflation. It's a political average.

What the official inflation index actually measures — and what it misses

Official inflation indices are typically built from a basket of goods and services consumed by households within a specific income band, in specific metropolitan regions. That alone reveals the first bias: anyone living outside those regions, with a different income, or with consumption patterns outside that basket isn't being measured accurately.

But the problem goes beyond sampling. There are methodological components that tend to soften perceived inflation:

Product substitution

If steak got expensive and the family switched to a cheaper cut, the index may register that "meat" rose less — because the composition of what families buy changed. Real purchasing power fell (you no longer eat steak), but the index records a smaller increase.

Hedonic adjustment

If a phone got 20% more expensive but the statistics agency considers it "15% better in quality," the index computes only a 5% rise. Your wallet paid 20% more. The index recorded 5%.

Politically weighted items

Electricity and fuel carry enormous weight in real cost of living — and both are directly influenced by subsidy policies and price controls. When a government artificially holds down gas prices ahead of an election, the official index falls. The inflationary pressure doesn't disappear — it's deferred.

The central point

Official inflation indices are a useful macroeconomic reference. But they were never designed to measure your specific family's inflation. Using the official index to deflate your investment return is like calculating your own body temperature using your city's average temperature.

Starting capital $100,000

Gap between the two readings $113,500

In the second scenario, the statement shows the portfolio tripling. But in real purchasing power — measured by the family's actual cost of living — the investor lost purchasing power over the decade. The number grew. The real wealth didn't.

Getting richer in nominal terms while getting poorer in purchasing power is the central paradox of any system that uses an inflatable currency as its unit of measurement for success.

Why this happens structurally

This isn't about banks or fund managers acting in bad faith. The issue is structural: when the unit of account — the dollar, the euro, the real — can be issued in unlimited quantities by a central bank's decision, it inevitably loses purchasing power over time. It's the dynamic of infinite money against finite goods and services.

By pricing everything in fiat currency, the system automatically anchors any return analysis to a ruler that's shrinking. Whoever earns more than official inflation preserves some purchasing power. Whoever earns less — or whose real inflation is higher than their return — loses purchasing power, even if the account balance never goes down.

The invisible tax: taxes on nominal gains

There's an aggravating factor rarely considered: income tax is charged on the nominal gain, not the real one. If you earned 12% nominally and inflation was 10%, you'll pay tax on the full 12% — even though your real gain was only about 1.8%. In some scenarios, it's mathematically possible to pay tax on a return that, in real terms, was negative.

Practical example

Nominal return: 12%. Tax (15% rate): −1.8%. Net return: 10.2%. Estimated real inflation: 13%. Real result after tax: −2.5%. The statement shows growth. Purchasing power fell.

How Bitcoin changes the unit of measurement

An alternative that has grown among investors thinking about long-term purchasing-power preservation is precisely to change the unit of account. Instead of asking "did my wealth grow in dollars?", the question becomes: "did my fraction of the global monetary supply grow?"

Bitcoin has a maximum supply of 21 million units — and that rule is set by code, not by decree. That means, unlike any fiat currency, the unit can't be diluted by political decision. Evaluating your wealth in BTC, or tracking your investment performance in terms of purchasing power in BTC, offers a completely different perspective from what statements denominated in fiat currency present.

Pricing in Fiat

  • Unit of account issued with no limit
  • Inflation silently erodes returns
  • Official inflation may not reflect your reality
  • Tax applies to nominal gains
  • A positive real return requires beating real inflation

Pricing in BTC

  • Maximum supply of 21 million — immutable
  • Can't be diluted by monetary policy
  • Growth measured as a fraction of a fixed supply
  • Structural protection against monetary expansion
  • A unit of measurement that doesn't shrink

This doesn't mean Bitcoin isn't volatile — it is, significantly so in the short term. The point here is epistemological: which ruler are you using to measure your wealth? One that shrinks every year, or one with a fixed length defined by math?

What to do with this information

The takeaway here isn't necessarily "exit every traditional investment." It's simpler and more powerful: demand clarity on what the return actually represents. When a bank manager or advisor presents a product with "12% a year," ask:

The questions your statement doesn't answer

→ Is this return gross or net of taxes?

→ Which inflation figure is being used to calculate the real gain?

→ Does that index reflect my family's actual cost of living?

→ In terms of real purchasing power, am I gaining or losing?

→ Does any portion of my wealth sit in an asset that can't be diluted?

There's no perfect investment. But there's a fundamental difference between investing while understanding reality — with its distortions, its limits, and its risks — and investing while believing the number on the statement tells the whole story.

The ruler may be shrinking. Knowing that is already a huge step.
