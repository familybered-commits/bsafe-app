It's not the bread that got expensive. It's the dollar that got small.

Most people look at rising prices and think "everything is more expensive." But there's a far more precise — and far more useful — way to understand what's actually happening.

📚 Financial Education ⏱ 6 min read 🟠 bsafebitcoin.org

Stop for a second and think about how you normally describe inflation. Probably something like: "meat is expensive," "rent is expensive," "everything is expensive." It's the natural way to phrase it — but it's also a phrasing that puts the problem in the wrong place.

Today's meat isn't more nutritious, rarer, or harder to produce than it was ten years ago. Real estate hasn't become more useful. A liter of gasoline hasn't changed composition. What changed is something else entirely: the ruler you're using to measure the value of things has shrunk.

Common view

"Things are getting more expensive."

→
Precise view

"My money is buying less and less."

The shrinking ruler

Imagine you measure people's height with a tape measure. Except every year, someone shrinks that tape measure a little — without telling anyone. People stay the same height, but with the smaller tape, everyone "appears" to be growing. Every time you measure someone, the number goes up — not because the person grew, but because your measuring tool shrank.

The core point

The dollar, the euro, and any other fiat currency are that tape measure. When the government issues more currency than the economy produces in new goods and services, each unit of that currency comes to represent a smaller fraction of the world's real wealth. The price of goods, measured in that currency, goes up — not because the goods are worth more, but because the currency is worth less.

A concrete example: the price of bread

Think about a loaf of bread. In terms of flour, yeast, energy, and labor needed to produce it, that loaf is, at its core, basically the same product it was decades ago. What changes is how many units of local currency you need to hand over to get it:

What stays the same What changes
The bread (flour, yeast, labor, energy) The amount of currency needed to buy it
The real value of the producer's labor The purchasing power of each currency unit
The product's usefulness in your life The number printed on the price tag

The bread didn't "decide" to be worth more. The currency is what got diluted — and the more units of it exist in circulation, the less each unit represents of the real wealth available in the world.

Why this shift in perspective matters

This isn't just a philosophical, semantic question. Changing how you see this completely changes the decisions you make with your money:

If you think "things are getting expensive," the natural reaction is to try to buy less, wait, cut spending, and keep whatever's left in a checking or savings account — hoping prices "come back down" eventually.

If you understand that "my money is losing value over time," the logic flips: holding cash in a currency that's being diluted means, in practice, losing purchasing power every single day — even if the number in your account never drops. The question stops being "what's going to get expensive?" and becomes "which asset can I store value in that can't be diluted?"

Your account balance can stay exactly the same for years — and you can still be poorer. Because what matters isn't the number, it's what that number can buy.

Where Bitcoin fits into this story

This is exactly where the comparison with Bitcoin becomes useful. Unlike the dollar, the euro, or any currency issued by a central bank, Bitcoin has a fixed maximum supply of 21 million units, defined in code since 2009 and impossible to change by political decision.

That means that while the "tape measure" of fiat currencies keeps shrinking year after year, Bitcoin's unit of measurement stays fixed. That's why, when the price of goods, real estate, or even other currencies is measured in Bitcoin over long cycles, the pattern that emerges is precisely the opposite of what we see with the dollar: the number tends to fall, because it's the unit of measurement that's staying scarce — and everything else is being measured in currencies that dilute.

This doesn't mean Bitcoin's dollar price doesn't fluctuate in the short term — it does, and quite a lot. But the underlying thesis is different from any fiat currency's thesis: on one side, a unit of account with growing, unlimited supply; on the other, one with fixed supply, auditable by anyone.

Tracking this in practice

One way to see this phenomenon play out in your own financial life is to track, over time, how much of your wealth sits in fixed-supply assets (like Bitcoin) versus how much sits in currency losing purchasing power. Comparing the evolution of your average entry price in dollars and local currency against other assets — gold, bonds, the S&P 500, a local stock index — helps make visible, with real numbers, what normally stays hidden inside "the price of things."

What to do with this information

Next time you feel like "everything is expensive," it's worth making this small mental swap: instead of asking "why did this get so expensive?", ask "what happened to my money that it now needs more units to buy the same thing?"

This isn't investment advice, nor a promise of returns — it's an invitation to reflect on how we measure value and on the structural incentives of each monetary system. It's up to each person to study, understand the risks, and make their own decisions responsibly.
